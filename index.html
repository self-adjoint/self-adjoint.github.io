This is the homepage of self-adjoint

What does "self-adjoint" mean? For matrices, it means the same as "Hermitian", i.e. a matrix that is equal to its conjugate transpose: $A^{\textsf{H}}=A$. All of the Pauli matrices are self-adjoint: $$ \sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$ More generally, for operators on a Hilbert space, it refers to an operator that is equal to its adjoint. The position and momentum operators are self-adjoint: $$ \widehat{x}\psi(x) = x\psi(x) \qquad \widehat{p}\psi(x) = -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}\psi(x) $$ The less mathematically inclined physicists might know these as observables.

Even more generally, for elements of a *-algebra, it refers to an element of the algebra that is equal to its adjoint, i.e. a fixed point of the * operation. This is too abstract for practical use.

Stirling's Formula

As $n\to\infty$, the following asymptotic relation holds: $$ n! \sim \sqrt{2\pi n}\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n $$

In 1730, Abraham de Moivre (1667 ‚Äì 1754) showed that as $n\to\infty$, $n! \sim Cn^{n+\frac{1}{2}}e^{-n}$ for some constant $C$, and later that year, James Stirling (1692 ‚Äì 1770) showed that $C=\sqrt{2\pi}$.
