This is the homepage of self-adjoint

What does "self-adjoint" mean? For matrices, it means the same as "Hermitian", i.e. a matrix that is equal to its conjugate transpose: $A^{\textsf{H}}=A$. All of the Pauli matrices are self-adjoint: $$ \sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix} \qquad \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$ More generally, for operators on a Hilbert space, it refers to an operator that is equal to its adjoint. The position and momentum operators are self-adjoint: $$ \widehat{x}\psi(x) = x\psi(x) \qquad \widehat{p}\psi(x) = -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}\psi(x) $$ The less mathematically inclined physicists might know these as observables.

Even more generally, for elements of a *-algebra, it refers to an element of the algebra that is equal to its adjoint, i.e. a fixed point of the * operation. This is too abstract for practical use.

Wallis' Product

$$ \frac{2}{1} \cdot \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{4}{3} \cdot \frac{4}{5} \cdot \frac{6}{5} \cdot \frac{6}{7} \cdot \frac{8}{7} \cdot \frac{8}{9} \cdots = \frac{\pi}{2} $$

This was first discovered in 1656 by John Wallis (1616 ‚Äì 1703), and is the first known expression for $\pi$ as an infinite product of fractions.

Stirling's Formula

As $n\to\infty$, the following asymptotic relation holds: $$ n! \sim \sqrt{2\pi n}\left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n $$

In 1730, Abraham de Moivre (1667 ‚Äì 1754) showed that as $n\to\infty$, $n! \sim Cn^{n+\frac{1}{2}}e^{-n}$ for some constant $C$, and later that year, James Stirling (1692 ‚Äì 1770) showed that $C=\sqrt{2\pi}$.

See here for a proof.

Corollaries

  • $\lim_{n\to\infty} \sqrt[n]{n!}=\infty$, so by the root test, the series $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^n}{n!}$ converges for all $z\in\mathbb{C}$.
  • The central binomial coefficient $\binom{2n}{n}=\frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2}$ satisfies $\binom{2n}{n}\sim\frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}}$ as $n\to\infty$.
  • Carleman's inequality

Gaussian Integral

$$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx = \sqrt{\pi} $$

This was first published in 1809 by Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777 ‚Äì 1855), though its discovery is usually attributed to Abraham de Moivre (1667 ‚Äì 1754) or Pierre-Simon Laplace (1749 ‚Äì 1827).

Statisticians may have trouble recognizing this formula due to its relative obscurity. In their language, it would be written as $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{x^2}{2}}\,dx = 1$.

It's not normal to know why the normalization constant in the normal distribution is $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$, and now you can impress your normal friends.
